This interactive two-page Power BI dashboard is constructed using the PanCan PDMR PDX Models 2025 dataset, a publicly available catalogue of 6,272 patient-derived xenograft (PDX) cancer samples derived from 950 unique patients across 39 distinct cancer types. Patient-derived xenograft models have been established as a clinically relevant platform for preclinical oncology research, given their capacity to preserve the genomic and histopathological characteristics of the originating tumor (Hidalgo et al., 2014). The dashboard is designed to support a director of translational research at an oncology drug discovery institute in making informed portfolio decisions regarding which biological models her team should prioritize for downstream experimental work.

The first page, Portfolio Overview, establishes the strategic context of the model library: the number of represented patients, samples, cancer types, and tumor sites, along with the library's distributional profile across mutation burden, anatomical site, and inferred ancestry. The second page, Biomarker Deep Dive, interrogates the biological signals most consequential for therapeutic relevance: microsatellite instability (MSI) status, mutation count, genome instability, and homologous recombination deficiency (HRD). The selection of these biomarkers reflects their well-documented roles in predicting response to targeted and immunotherapeutic agents (Le et al., 2015; Konstantinopoulos et al., 2015).
The principal finding is that the library is substantially skewed along three dimensions: cancer-type representation is dominated by colorectal cancer at approximately one in five samples, mutation burden concentrates in the intermediate range, and inferred ancestry is overwhelmingly European at approximately 71%. Each of these asymmetries carries direct implications for the generalizability of any therapeutic insight derived from this collection. The underrepresentation of non-European ancestries in particular reflects a broader and well-documented inequity in biomedical research infrastructure (Popejoy & Fullerton, 2016).




Business Questions Addressed:



How many distinct patients and samples are available, and across which cancer types? What proportion of samples are MSI-H (microsatellite-instability high), typically the most therapeutically tractable?

How is the mutation burden distributed: are we biased toward hypermutated tumors? Is the ancestry distribution diverse enough to support generalizable findings? Where are the gaps in recalcitrant or rare cancer coverage? Which cancer types harbor the most MSI-H samples?
